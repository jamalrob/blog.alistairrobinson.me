I like this novella very much, so if parts of my review seem harsh it's only because I found the story so stimulating. When I say it's a beautiful but ultimately tragic lament culminating in an all-too-cosy retreat to the comforts of Western liberalism, I'm not consigning it to the flames. It is well worth reading, perhaps even up there with _The Dispossessed_ and _The Left hand of Darkness_.

## The world and the story

It is set on an Earth-like planet of ocean and forest, in the early years of its colonization by humans from Earth, who have named it New Tahiti. Until now it has not known industry, agriculture, environmental exploitation, oppression, or social conflict. It is a pristine wilderness, all land covered in a forest of oak, ash, hazel, cedar, etc. (it turns out that the planet was seeded a long time ago by the super-advanced Hainish civilization with the same plant and animal progenitors as Earth was).

Earth, we learn, has been reduced to a toxic dump; New Tahiti represents something of a new start. And the first order of business is to cut down the trees and ship them back to Earth, where timber is worth more than gold. Next, the idea is to farm the land intensively and create a world fit for Terrans. Whether stripping the forests is sustainable and whether the native inhabitants are 

The planet is home to an intelligent but apparently passive species, the Athsheans---the planet's local name is Athshe---who are known by the colonizers as "creechies." Most of the Terrans, led by the sadistic Captain Davidson, see the native people as subhuman and feel fully entitled to use them as slave labour in their logging operations---and to abuse, rape, and murder them when they please. 

I said it was a pristine wilderness. But when the story moves away from the logging operations and into the forest to focus on the Athsheans, it comes to feel more like a _garden_. This is a peace-loving people with complex traditions that successfully ensure harmonious living, both among themselves and in their environment. 

We are gradually introduced to the rich life of the Athsheans through the experiences of Lyubov, the colony's anthropologist. And finally the focus moves to Selver, an Athshean who decides to break with his people's traditional taboos against violence to lead his people into violent resistance.

## Preachy

So the first impression is of a simple anti-imperialist allegory for the Vietnam War, and simultaneously for environmental destruction. Le Guin is angered and outraged by the artrocities perpetrated by the US in Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia. She does not flinch from showing us the horrors and abuses. When she is not shocking us into anger she is moving us to tears for the suffering of the innocent people.

But it felt too on-the-nose, too manichean---the bad guys too one-dimensionally bad, the good guys too cuddly and innocent. There seemed to be none of the complexity of _The Dispossessed_, in which the anarchist planet, though the one that gets Le Guin's sympathy, is no paradise.

That this works is a testament to Le Guin's abilities. 



I'm fairly allergic to fiction with a message, but somehow, she won me over. I say somehow, but I know how: it's because she is a great writer, controlled, generous, intelligent and humane.

## Problems

Much as I love the story, it has problems, and I think they ultimately undermine its apparent radicalism. My interpretation is at risk of being extreme, of being an uncharitable exaggeration. My only reply to that is a non-reply: as Adorno used to say, only exaggeration is true.

In a nutshell, the Athsheans are victims to be pitied, not historical agents to be joined in solidarity; when it comes to the crunch, the reader is expected to follow Le Guin herself in identifying primarily with the benevolent Hainish overseers, who represent the enlightened liberalism in which Le Guin and most of her readers feel at home. The Athsheans remain fully Other---once exotic, naive and primitive, later tragically corrupted by an insufficiently liberal Terran humanity. 

[expand]

This deep problem comes to the surface in the character of Captain Davidson. It is troublesome enough that he's a cartoon villain, a figure of absololute evil, but see how he functions---as the bad apple. In his _individual_ wickedness he functions as the convenient exculpation for _structural_ wickedness. The more radical critique of colonialism would attack the latter. As it is, the attack is softened: insofar as imperialism and colonialism are bad, they are bad because they provides space for evil psychopaths to exploit and abuse the weak.

For those who believe that imperialism and colonialism are _intrinsically_ exploitative and abusive, this is disappointing.

### A note on hard science fiction

Some commentators on SF literature use the term "hard SF" to designate fiction with lots of physics and mathematics---maybe the space opera of Vernor Vinge is a good example. For a long time I've denounced this as a wayward usage, one that diverges from the term's traditional meaning.

Although anthropology and sociology are sometimes regarded as soft sciences, in contrast with the hard sciences of physics, chemistry, and biology, I don't think soft and hard in science fiction have traditionally aligned with that distinction. Hardness in science fiction doesn't mean "physics, not sociology." Rather, it means disciplined speculation grounded in a systematic understanding of reality. We assign the label of _hard SF_ to a story to the extent that it imagines developments and discoveries in science, whether the science is anthropology or fundamental physics.

There is evidence for this in the history of the genre. I don't think it's controversial to say that one of the exemplars of classic hard science fiction is Isaac Asimov, and his most famous creation is the _Foundation_ series, which centres around a fictitious science called _psychohistory_. All the science in these books is _social_ science.

If this is right, Asimov, Vinge, _and_ Le Guin are all hard SF.

But I've changed my mind. From the instinct that Le Guin ought to be taken seriously as a writer whose speculations are intelligently extrapolated from real-life systematic knowledge, there are two ways to go. I could say, according to the argument I've just set out, that she is hard SF after all and everyone these days is misusing the term. 

Or I could say that hard SF is not scientifically serious enough to contain her: _hard SF is not hard enough_.

Although Asimov's _Foundation_ centres around social science, it is a _mathematized_ social science, that is, a social science supposedly raised up to the level of physics and thereby _hardened_. It reminds me of Engineer's Syndrome, which...

> refers to the tendency of people with an engineering mindset (thinking about things from a technical angle and often in terms of systems) to believe that because they’re really, really good at engineering-related tasks, they are also good at (and understand) everything else.
>
> --- Colin Wright ([Engineer's Disease](https://brainlenses.substack.com/p/engineers-disease))

Such thinking is usually wrong. In Asimov's case I'd say that the prediction of history and human behaviour with mathematics was little more than an Enlightenment hope with no empirical support, born at the moment when other sciences suggested that even chaos might yield to calculation. The very idea is an inheritance of nineteenth-century positivism---a mashup of Auguste Comte and James Clerk Maxwell. People are particles or molecules, societies are thermodynamic systems, and history is governed by mathematical probability. Inconvenient human reality just disappears if you zoom out far enough.

Le Guin does not indulge in such naive Enlightenment fantasies. Her speculative social science is mature enough to reflect _real_ social science, facing up to the considerable unknowability of human systems. Thus her fiction is arguably superior even just in hard SF terms: the speculation is grounded actual science, abandoning the positivism that was discredited and already abandoned by modern sociologists and anthropologists.

Of course, it comes down to politics. In rejecting the Golden Age's Enlightenment fantasies, Le Guin's fiction also rejects the technocratic optimism of mid-century American modernity and its underlying ideology of manifest destiny. But more deeply, she rejects the deep urge for mastery engendered by the Enlightenment (as Adorno and many others have argued).

It's true that Le Guin goes in for the classic hard SF tropes herself. Functioning in literary terms a lot like faster-than-light travel,  _The Word for World is Forest_ and her other Hainish books feature the _ansible_, a device that allows instant communication across any distance. Even when a science-fictional device is not disciplined by plausible extrapolation from existing knowledge, it need not be dismissed as "mere" escapism. In fact, Le Guin defended escapism more than once. For example, she wrote that "The direction of escape is toward freedom" ([here](https://www.ursulakleguin.com/blog/28-it-doesnt-have-to-be-the-way-it-is)). The big difference is that Golden Age science fiction and hard SF were usually providing escape, not towards freedom, but into fantasies of triumph and control.
