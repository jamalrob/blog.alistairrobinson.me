I like this novella very much, so if parts of my review seem harsh it's only because I found the story so stimulating. When I say it's a beautiful but ultimately tragic lament culminating in an all-too-cosy retreat to the comforts of Western liberalism, I'm not consigning it to the flames. It is well worth reading, perhaps even up there with _The Dispossessed_ and _The Left hand of Darkness_.

## The world and the story

It is set on an Earth-like planet of ocean and forest, in the early years of its colonization by humans from Earth (the Terrans), who have named it New Tahiti. Until now it has not known industry, agriculture, environmental exploitation, oppression, or social conflict. It is a pristine wilderness, all land covered in a forest of oak, ash, hazel, cedar, etc.---it turns out that the planet was seeded a long time ago by the super-advanced Hainish civilization with the same plant and animal progenitors as Earth was.

Earth, we learn, has been reduced to a toxic dump; New Tahiti represents something of a new start. But the first order of business is to cut down the trees and ship them back to Earth, where timber is worth more than gold. Next, the plan is to farm the cleared land intensively and create a world fit for Terrans. Whether stripping the forests will lead to sustainable agriculture, and whether the native inhabitants are happy with all this---the Terrans are mostly unconcerned with these questions. 

Among those native inhabitants is an intelligent but apparently passive species, the Athsheans---the planet's local name is Athshe---who are known by the colonizers as "creechies." Most of the Terrans, led by the sadistic Captain Davidson, see the native people as subhuman and feel fully entitled to use them as slave labour in their logging operations---and to abuse, rape, and murder them when they please. When Raj Lyubov, the colony's anthropologist, points out that the "creechies" are actually close cousins of ours, descended from the same original Hainish seed species, they don't want to know.

I said it was a pristine wilderness. But when the story moves away from the logging operations and into the forest to focus on the Athsheans, it comes to feel more like a _garden_. This is a peace-loving people with complex traditions that successfully ensure harmonious living, both among themselves and in their environment. 

We are gradually introduced to the rich life of the Athsheans through Lyubov, whose anthropological work has brought him to a fascinated appreciation of this simultaneously familiar and strange people.

Later, the focus moves to Selver, an Athshean who decides to break with his people's traditional taboos against violence to lead his people into violent resistance.

## Preachy

So the first impression is of a simple anti-imperialist allegory for the Vietnam War, and simultaneously for environmental destruction. Le Guin is angered and outraged by the artrocities perpetrated by the US in Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia. She does not flinch from showing us the horrors and abuses. When she is not shocking us into anger she is moving us to tears for the suffering of innocent people.

For a moment I worried that it would turn out to be too on-the-nose, too manichean---the bad guys too one-dimensionally bad, the good guys too cuddly and innocent. There seemed to be none of the complexity of _The Dispossessed_, in which the anarchist planet, though the one that gets Le Guin's sympathy, is no paradise. This, by contrast, felt like a hippie fantasy.

But it's not that. Complexity creeps in as we progress through the story. I'm allergic to fiction with a message, but somehow she won me over. I say somehow, but I know how. It's her writing: masterful, controlled, intelligent and humane. And, surprisingly for such a polemical work, _subtle_. The allegory is explicit, the anti-imperialist points made with hammer-blows, but by the end the feeling is of a complex story that manages to do much more in a hundred pages than many more recent SF authors do in three or four hundred.

## Captain Davidson

Davidson is a cartoon villain. Le Guin knew this when writing it, but she allowed her unconscious to go where it wanted, as she explains in the introduction:

> But Davidson is, though not uncomplex, pure; he is purely evil — and I don’t, consciously, believe purely evil people exist. But my unconscious has other opinions.

"Not uncomplex," if you're really paying attention, perhaps. But he does come across as a one-dimensional antagonist. Again, it's a testament to her writing that we don't perceive this as detracting from the story.

As someone said the other day on TPF:

> I'm not sure how she gets away with it, but I think part of it is that she's so smart, you trust her and want to listen. We're used to preaching from people who shouldn't — so there's a sort of double offense — but I just don't seem to mind being preached to by her, which is an odd experience. 
>
> --- [Srap Tasmaner](https://thephilosophyforum.com/discussion/comment/1038151)

## Problems

Excellent as the story is, I have criticisms. The problems run deep, ultimately undermining any apparent political radicalism. I realize that my interpretation risks being an uncharitable exaggeration; my only reply to that is a non-reply: as Adorno used to say, only exaggeration is true.

In a nutshell, the Athsheans are victims to be pitied, not historical agents to be joined in solidarity. When it comes to the crunch, the reader is expected to follow Le Guin herself in identifying primarily with the benevolent Hainish overseers, who represent the enlightened liberalism in which Le Guin and most of her readers feel at home. The Athsheans remain fully Other---once exotic, naive and primitive, later tragically corrupted by an insufficiently liberal Terran humanity. In the end, the Hainish---who I'm tempted to refer to as the galaxy's liberal elite---are the only real hope. 

These complaints might seem a bit abstract, but the issues become concrete in the character of Captain Davidson. That he's a cartoon villain, a figure of absololute evil, might be bad enough from a literary point of view, but it's worse than that. Look at how he functions---as the bad apple. In his _individual_ wickedness he is the convenient exculpation for _structural_ wickedness. A more radical critique of colonialism would attack the latter. As it is, any attack is softened: insofar as imperialism and colonialism are bad, they are bad because they provide a space, far removed from the constraints of liberal civilization, for evil psychopaths to exploit and abuse the weak. For a substantial part of the book we are encouraged to think,  _once the ansible keeps the dumb nasty soldiers under the control of the intellectuals, everything will be fine._

For those who believe that imperialism and colonialism are _intrinsically_ exploitative and abusive, not _accidentally_ so owing to a few bad actors, this is disappointing. A Marxist would say that it perfectly demonstrates the weakness of anarchism---the politics Le Guin was most sympathetic to---, which relies on liberal moralism and thus fails to sustain a radical critique of the status quo. This is exactly what we see in _The Word for World is Forest_: the horrors of oppression and exploitation brilliantly evoked, then a failure to transform this justified moral outrage into a systemic critique of imperialism or social domination more generally.

"A Marxist would likely say" is a cop-out, of course, but it signals something real, namely the discomfort I feel passing down sectarian judgements on works of art. This is not only because the Marxist mode of thought feels a bit worn-out and that Le Guin is on my side, but also because it is barbaric to condemn this or any other work of literature for its lack of political correctness (in the old Soviet sense). One shouldn't expect to have one's politics confirmed by every work of literature. 

So I'll leave the politics there and move on to world-building.

## Invention

For such a short allegorical polemic, it contains a lot of inventive world-building.  

1. touch
2. language (spiritual vs. bureaucratic)
3. dream-time

The science of this kind of science fiction is anthropology.

## Hard science fiction?

Some commentators on SF literature use the term "hard SF" to designate fiction with lots of physics and mathematics---maybe the space opera of Vernor Vinge is a good example. For a long time I've denounced this as a wayward usage, one that diverges from the term's traditional meaning.

Although anthropology and sociology are sometimes regarded as soft sciences, in contrast with the hard sciences of physics, chemistry, and biology, I don't think soft and hard in science fiction have traditionally aligned with that distinction. Hardness in science fiction doesn't mean "physics, not sociology." Rather, it means disciplined speculation grounded in a systematic understanding of reality. We assign the label of "hard SF" to a story when its imagined developments and discoveries are grounded in science, whether the science is anthropology or fundamental physics.

There is evidence for this in the history of the genre. I don't think it's controversial to say that one of the exemplars of classic hard SF is Isaac Asimov, and his most famous creation is the _Foundation_ series, which centres around a fictitious science called _psychohistory_. All the science in these books is _social_ science.

If this is right, Asimov, Vinge, _and_ Le Guin are all hard SF.

But I've changed my mind. From the instinct that Le Guin ought to be taken seriously as a writer whose speculations are intelligently extrapolated from real-life systematic knowledge, there are two ways to go. I could say, according to the argument I've just set out, that she is hard SF after all and everyone these days is misusing the term. 

Or I could say that hard SF is not scientifically serious enough to contain her: _hard SF is not hard enough_.

Although Asimov's _Foundation_ centres around social science, it is a _mathematized_ social science, that is, a social science supposedly raised up to the level of physics and thereby _hardened_. It reminds me of Engineer's Syndrome, which...

> refers to the tendency of people with an engineering mindset (thinking about things from a technical angle and often in terms of systems) to believe that because they’re really, really good at engineering-related tasks, they are also good at (and understand) everything else.
>
> --- Colin Wright ([Engineer's Disease](https://brainlenses.substack.com/p/engineers-disease))

Such thinking is usually wrong. In Asimov's case I'd say that the prediction of history and human behaviour using mathematics is little more than an Enlightenment hope with no empirical support, originating at the moment when the zeitgeist suggested that even chaos might yield to calculation. It is an inheritance of nineteenth-century positivism, a mashup of Auguste Comte and James Clerk Maxwell. People are particles or molecules, societies are thermodynamic systems, and history is governed by mathematical probability. Inconvenient human reality just disappears if you zoom out far enough.

Le Guin does not indulge in such naive Enlightenment fantasies. Her speculative social science is mature enough to reflect _real_ social science, facing up to the considerable unknowability of human systems. Thus her fiction is arguably superior even just in hard SF terms: the speculation is grounded actual science, abandoning the positivism that was discredited and already abandoned by modern sociologists and anthropologists.

Of course, it comes down to politics. In rejecting the Golden Age's Enlightenment fantasies, Le Guin's fiction also rejects the technocratic optimism of mid-century American modernity and its underlying ideology of manifest destiny. But more deeply, she rejects the deep urge for mastery engendered by the Enlightenment (as Adorno and many others have argued).

It's true that Le Guin goes in for the classic hard SF tropes herself. Functioning in literary terms a lot like faster-than-light travel,  _The Word for World is Forest_ and her other Hainish books feature the _ansible_, a device that allows instant communication across any distance. Even when a science-fictional device is not disciplined by plausible extrapolation from existing knowledge, it need not be dismissed as "mere" escapism. In fact, Le Guin defended escapism more than once. For example, she wrote that "The direction of escape is toward freedom" ([here](https://www.ursulakleguin.com/blog/28-it-doesnt-have-to-be-the-way-it-is)). The big difference is that Golden Age science fiction and hard SF were usually providing escape, not towards freedom, but into fantasies of triumph and control.
