### 1.1. Glaucon is unconvinced (357a-357b)

Socrates thinks the discussion is over (at least, that's what he says), but Glaucon, speaking for the others, objects:

> Do you want to *seem* to have persuaded us, Socrates, that it is better in every way to be just rather than unjust, or do you want to *really* persuade us of this?

What was so unconvincing about Socrates's arguments in Book I? There are two main problems.

Firstly, Socrates argues that a ruler _qua_ ruler acts for the benefit of the ruled, and therefore that Thrasymachus's definition of justice is wrong. But his argument rests on an assumed concept of craft, namely the functional concept. Thrasymachus holds an equally plausible concept of craft, namely the instrumental concept, and he is defeated only because Socrates outperforms him in the back and forth of dialectic. So if the others are left dissatisfied, it's for a good reason: the position that Thrasymachus represents was not defeated.

Secondly, Socrates also argues that justice is more personally beneficial than injustice. But he was able to carry this through only because Thrasymachus agreed too quickly to one of Socrates's crucial moves:

> SOCRATES: Now, didn’t we agree that justice is a soul’s virtue and injustice its vice?  
>
> THRASYMACHUS: Yes, we did agree.
>
> --- [353e]

But that is not actually what was agreed before:

> SOCRATES: A just person has turned out to be good and wise, then, and an unjust one ignorant and bad.
>
> _Thrasymachus agreed to all this_ ...
>
> --- [350c]

Socrates has shown that a just person tends to be good and wise, but he hasn't secured the premise that justice is the soul's excellence (virtue or *arete*) that allows it to perform the soul's function (*ergon*) well, i.e., to enable living well and therefore happiness. Presumably *something* is the soul's virtue that makes this possible, i.e., its *proper virtue*, but Socrates hasn't established that it's justice. It could be wisdom. That is, wisdom could be the excellence of the soul that enables a person to live well, while justice is just a by-product.

This would mean that justice, though a virtue, is not thereby a good in itself. And if justice were merely instrumentally good---while wisdom was the real excellence, good in itself---then someone sufficiently wise might be able to make *injustice* work for them even better than justice. And this is almost exactly what Thrasymachus argued.

### 1.6. Adeimantus reinforces the challenge (362d-367e)

Whereas Glaucon's argument was about what people *do*, Adeimantus looks at what people *say*. He shows that even when *praising* justice, people do so for extrinsic consequences. It is not as if the merely extrinsic benefits of justice---a good reputation, primarily---constitute a dirty secret; everyone is quite open about it, including the best poets. It's as if nobody can even imagine justice as intrinsically good, as opposed to instrumentally good, or good in its extrinsic rewards.
